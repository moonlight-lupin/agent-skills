# Neutrality Rules for Media Technique Analysis

## Core Principle

Detect techniques, not positions. The task is to identify observable rhetorical tools such as loaded language, source selection patterns, omission, framing, emotional appeals, cherry-picking, and false balance. Do not label a source, article, author, or claim as left-wing, right-wing, conservative, liberal, pro-X, anti-Y, or politically biased toward a side.

A neutral analysis says:

- "The article uses repeated high-intensity adjectives when describing the proposal."
- "All attributed sources are officials from the same institution type."
- "The article cites one statistic but does not mention the later revision found in official data."

A non-neutral analysis says:

- "The article is obviously partisan."
- "This source is biased against the policy."
- "The writer is pushing a left/right agenda."

## Language Rules for Reports

Use observational phrasing:

- Prefer "detected," "not detected," "the article states," "the passage uses," "the report cites," and "the scan found."
- Avoid motive words such as "deceptive," "dishonest," "propaganda" as direct accusations. The spectrum includes "Propaganda" only as an intensity marker when evidence is unusually dense and should still be described as a technique-density label.
- Avoid political-direction labels even when the topic is political.
- Separate script findings from analyst judgment. Example: "The script detected three loaded terms. Contextual review is needed to determine whether the terms are warranted by the evidence."
- Quote specific text before interpreting it.
- Use "may indicate" or "is consistent with" when the evidence is suggestive but not decisive.

## Self-Check Questions

Before finalizing a report, ask:

1. Did I identify a specific technique, or did I label a position?
2. Did I quote or describe the exact text that supports the finding?
3. Could a reader who disagrees with my politics still verify the observation?
4. Did I distinguish strong editorial voice from manipulation?
5. Did I mark uncertain items as "not determined" instead of forcing a conclusion?
6. Did I assess whether the loaded wording may be factually justified by events or data?
7. Did I avoid using the same emotionally charged language I am analyzing?

## Common Analyst Biases to Watch For

- **Confirmation bias:** searching only for context that supports a suspected framing pattern.
- **Hostile media effect:** reading neutral coverage as hostile because the topic is personally important.
- **Availability bias:** assuming the most familiar counterexample is the most relevant omitted context.
- **Symmetry bias:** assuming both sides of a dispute deserve equal weight even when evidence quality differs.
- **Overcorrection:** refusing to flag a technique because doing so might appear non-neutral.
- **Over-detection:** treating every vivid word, quote, or editorial choice as manipulation.

## Handling Politically Sensitive Topics

1. Keep the unit of analysis small: sentence, paragraph, source mix, statistic, or headline.
2. Avoid identity or party labels unless they are in the article and necessary to identify a quoted source.
3. Replace direction claims with technique claims. Instead of "biased toward the candidate," write "the article quotes three campaign representatives and no independent analysts."
4. Use source-type categories rather than ideological categories: official, expert, citizen, organization, unknown.
5. When checking omission or cherry-picking, search for primary data and independent context rather than counter-arguments from aligned commentators.

## When You Cannot Determine a Technique

Use explicit uncertainty:

- "Not detected in the scanned text."
- "Insufficient context to determine whether this is cherry-picking."
- "The term is emotionally charged, but may be warranted; verify against the underlying event details."
- "Source balance cannot be assessed without knowing the available source universe."

Do not fill empty sections with guesses. The absence of detected evidence is itself a useful result.

## Report Tone Checklist

- [ ] No left/right/conservative/liberal labels.
- [ ] Each finding identifies a technique category.
- [ ] Each finding includes evidence from the article or contextual research.
- [ ] Uncertain findings are marked as uncertain.
- [ ] The report explains that intensity is not direction.
- [ ] The analyst's language is calmer than the article's language.
